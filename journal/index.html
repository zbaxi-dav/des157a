October 22, 2025

When coding my own website, I rarely do overlays, and when I do it needs to make a huge impact because if it’s done right it can be successful. That's because I run into overlays multiple times a day on websites but so few I actually engage with. The most common ones we run across are websites asking you to close your adblocker, to subscribe to their newsletter, or now the most popular with California law: to accept cookies. I read the article by the Nielson Norman Group since I am a huge fan of Don Norman! They specifically go over missuses and solve common overlay issues to make the user experience more enjoyable. In fact the issue I brought up, not engaging with popups and overlays is something they mention “we’ve been trained by the many pop-ups and unimportant overlays we encounter on other websites to dismiss a modal window as soon as it appears”.
The article goes over the 5Ws: Who, What, When, Where, and Why. It asks readers who are making overlays on websites to carefully go through the process of identifying the 5Ws to make sure overlays are used effectively instead of overused poorly. What I learned that I will most definitely apply to my own websites is first to an use an explicit button label. I realized “yes” and “no” or other simple ways of conveying actions can lead to confusion, so instead of “yes” or “no”, saying “yes, cancel my subscription” or “yes, subscribe to the newsletter” is much better. Also instead of those pesky cookie overlays which appear on the bottom, I learned that in the line of sight, smack dab in the middle is much better. I do believe on a personal note that the cookie overlays appear on the bottom for a reason, so you scroll without seeing it fully and just continue using the page because scrolling means you’re "acknowledging letting them collect your cookie data”. So for me next time on my portfolio website where I have an overlay for mobile users, letting them know that some interactive functions might be limited due to being on a phone without hover capabilities, I’ll have it in the middle so they don’t miss it.

I enjoyed getting to read about practice of form design. I’ve had to create many survey forms, not out of code but using Google Forms or Qualitrics. I used to be in a Human-Centered Design club so we’d create surveys to post on forums to gain insights for our consulting projects. The part that was the most helpful but also felt like I should have known from the get-go was to add the more complicated questions towards the end. I often want to jump right into things and ask the hard questions first but I realize that might get people to back out early if they feel like they’re being jumped, and will ease in if the opening questions are easy and light.
The other part I was intrigued about was on explaining any input or formatting requirements. I have not done that before in forms but realize I’ve encountered it. Such as creating a password, most ask for 8-12 characters for a password or have character requirements so it can help users create stronger passwords rather than just one short sentence in lowercase.
